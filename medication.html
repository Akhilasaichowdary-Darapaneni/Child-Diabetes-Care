Insulin Basics

  • Many children with Type 1 diabetes need insulin every day, using pens, syringes, or pumps.
  • There are different types of insulin (short-acting, long-acting, etc.). Your doctor chooses a mix that suits your child.
  • Insulin helps sugar move from the blood into the bodyâ€™s cells for energy.
  • Missing insulin can lead to very high sugar and dangerous emergency situations.

Never change the type or dose of insulin without clear instructions from the healthcare team.

Storing and Handling Insulin

  • Keep unopened insulin in the fridge, away from the freezer section.
  • Once opened, follow the storage instructions on the label (often room temperature for a set number of days).
  • Do not use insulin that is expired, frozen, or looks cloudy when it should be clear.
  • When travelling, use a cool pouch to protect insulin from heat.

Injection and Pump Care

  • Rotate injection sites (arms, thighs, abdomen, buttocks) to avoid skin lumps.
  • Check for redness, swelling, or pain around injection or pump sites.
  • Keep the area clean and dry before placing a new needle or cannula.
  • If a site looks infected, call the doctor for advice.

Tablets and Other Medicines

  • Children with Type 2 diabetes may use tablets, insulin, or both.
  • Give tablets exactly as prescribed, at the right time and amount.
  • Tell the doctor about any other medicines or herbal products the child is taking.
  • Watch for side effects such as stomach upset, rashes, or unusual tiredness, and report them.

If you ever give the wrong dose by accident, contact your healthcare team or local emergency number to ask what to do next.
